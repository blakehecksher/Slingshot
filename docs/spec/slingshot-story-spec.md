# Slingshot — Story Spec

_Status: Draft_  
_Purpose: Give the game a coherent fiction that supports the core gravity mechanic, mining loop, ship design, and overall tone._

---

## 1. Core Story Premise

The asteroid field is valuable because it contains **Dead Iron**: a rare, ultra-dense gravitational material that distorts local space far beyond what its visible mass should allow.

Most asteroids in the field are ordinary rock. The dangerous ones are not. They contain buried veins, pockets, or compressed cores of Dead Iron. These deposits make certain asteroids behave like gravity wells, pulling ships off-course, bending projectiles, trapping careless pilots, and turning open space into a navigable terrain.

The field is not just where the game happens. The field is the road, the mine, the hazard, and the economy.

The central question remains:

> **How close can you get?**

---

## 2. The Situation

For a long time, the field was ignored as junk rock: too unstable for large-scale settlement, too remote for conventional mining, and too dangerous for standard survey crews.

Then early drones started disappearing.

Not exploding. Not being attacked. Just vanishing into bad trajectories. Some were later found crushed against ordinary-looking asteroids. Others were trapped in slow decaying orbits around rocks that should not have had enough mass to matter.

The first serious surveys discovered the cause: Dead Iron.

Dead Iron is ugly, heavy, rare, and valuable enough that people keep dying for it. It is used in advanced propulsion systems, gravity instrumentation, energy storage, weapons research, and industrial mass-control systems. Nobody fully agrees on what it is. Some call it a naturally occurring ultra-dense metal. Others think it is dimensionally compressed matter, exotic stellar debris, or something stranger that only looks like metal because human instruments are bad at naming new things.

The miners do not really care.

They get paid by the kilogram.

---

## 3. Tone and World Texture

This is not a clean sci-fi frontier. It is a working field.

The field should feel like a half-legal mining rush at the edge of known space. There are corporate claims, independent pilots, scavengers, old pros, wreck chasers, black-market buyers, repair crews, and patrol ships that enforce the rules only when paid to care.

The mood is functional, worn, and specific. Ships are not sleek hero machines. They are tools that have been modified, repaired, overloaded, and flown too hard. They look like they have jobs.

The base is not a grand space station. It is a practical foothold in low-gravity open space: fuel, repairs, trading, cargo weighing, debt, rumors, and bad coffee.

The deeper field is where the money is. It is also where good pilots die.

---

## 4. Dead Iron

### 4.1 What It Is

Dead Iron is a rare gravitational ore found inside certain asteroids. It appears as dark metallic veins, dense nodules, glassy inclusions, and buried cores. It may oxidize, scar, or discolor the surrounding rock, leaving visible signs on high-value asteroids.

It is not valuable because it is shiny. It is valuable because it behaves wrongly.

Dead Iron does not simply weigh a lot. When concentrated, compressed, or aligned in large enough masses, it produces gravitational effects disproportionate to its apparent size. A small sample can disrupt instruments. A large buried vein can bend a ship's trajectory. A compressed asteroid core can create a gravity well strong enough to trap an unskilled pilot.

The exact physics are not fully understood. The practical rule is simple:

> **Dead Iron is safest when dispersed and most dangerous when concentrated.**

### 4.2 Why the Asteroids Have Huge Gravity

The asteroids are not uniformly made of Dead Iron. Most are ordinary rock wrapped around abnormal cores or threaded with dense veins.

Over long periods of time, collisions, pressure, and slow accretion have compressed Dead Iron into pockets and cores. Those concentrations create the field's gravity wells.

This explains why two asteroids of similar visible size can behave very differently. One may be mostly rock. Another may contain a buried core dense enough to bend flight paths from a distance.

The pilots learn to read this the way sailors read currents.

### 4.3 Visual Language

Dead Iron-rich asteroids should be readable before the HUD explains them.

Possible signs:

- dark metallic seams across the asteroid surface
- exposed black or blue-black mineral faces
- glassy impact scars around dense deposits
- dust rings or small captured debris fields
- subtle orbiting fragments around high-gravity rocks
- warm instrument glow or scan shimmer near exposed veins
- surface cracks that imply something heavier inside

The strongest asteroids should feel ancient, heavy, and wrong.

---

## 5. Mining Logic

Mining in Slingshot is not conventional drilling. The ships do not land, deploy rigs, or cut whole cores out of asteroids.

They perform close-pass extraction.

A mining ship approaches a Dead Iron-rich asteroid, rides its gravity envelope, scans exposed veins, fractures small surface deposits, and collects loose ore as dust, flakes, slag, and small fragments. The ship must stay close enough for its collectors to work, but not so close that the gravity well captures it.

This makes flying the mining action.

The better the pilot, the closer and longer they can stay near a deposit. The closer and longer they stay, the more they collect.

### 5.1 Why Proximity Mining Makes Sense

Dead Iron is difficult to extract from long range because the valuable material is usually buried, shielded by rock, or exposed only in small unstable veins. The ship's mining systems are short-range by necessity.

A typical mining pass uses several systems at once:

- **Gravimetric scanners** locate dense deposits beneath the surface.
- **Pulse cutters** or small charges fracture exposed veins.
- **Magnetic intake fields** pull loose particles and fragments into the ship.
- **Containment pods** stabilize the collected material before it can clump or interfere with the ship.

The ship is not chewing through the asteroid. It is skimming value from the edge of danger.

---

## 6. Cargo and Containment

Dead Iron creates a useful fiction problem:

If the ore has abnormal gravitational behavior, why does carrying it not make the ship dangerously gravitational?

The answer:

> **Mining ships collect Dead Iron in small fragments and store it in powered containment pods that keep the material dispersed, separated, and damped.**

A ship is not carrying one giant core. It is carrying dust, flakes, fragments, and slag suspended inside industrial cargo pods. Each piece is manageable. The danger comes as those pieces accumulate.

### 6.1 Containment Pods

Dead Iron cargo pods do not erase mass. They are not magic anti-gravity boxes.

They are stabilizers.

The pods keep the ore separated so it cannot clump into a more dangerous concentration. They use magnetic suspension, vibration isolation, thermal control, and crude gravimetric damping to keep the cargo quiet.

A full pod is stable, but only barely.

As cargo increases, the dampers work harder. The ship becomes more sluggish, its systems strain, and nearby loose particles may begin to drift subtly toward it. A heavily loaded ship is not a black hole, but it is no longer neutral either.

The ship itself starts to become part of the gravitational problem.

### 6.2 Gameplay Implications — Future, Not Required Immediately

This does not need to become a Phase 1 mechanic, but it gives later systems a strong story foundation.

Possible implications:

- Full cargo slightly reduces acceleration or maneuverability.
- Full cargo makes escape from deep wells more dangerous.
- Damaged cargo pods may flicker or destabilize.
- Loose debris, dust, or spilled cargo may drift toward a heavily loaded ship.
- Enemy scanners may detect rich cargo ships more easily.
- Returning to base while full becomes tense for physical reasons, not just economic ones.

The important rule is that the cargo should feel valuable and dangerous.

A full hold should feel like money with a pulse.

---

## 7. The Field as Geography

The asteroid field naturally separates into zones based on Dead Iron concentration.

### 7.1 Open Space

Low gravity. Low resource density. Safer travel. This is where the base lives.

Open space is not empty, but it is navigable without much skill. It is the place where new pilots learn the ship and where damaged pilots limp home.

### 7.2 Mid Field

Moderate gravity. Moderate resources. Mixed asteroid types.

This is where early mining runs happen. Pilots begin learning to use gravity instead of fighting it. Slingshots become useful, but mistakes are usually recoverable.

### 7.3 Deep Field

High gravity. High resource concentration. Large Dead Iron cores.

This is where the money is. It is also where slow approaches fail. The biggest asteroids cannot be safely approached like static objects. A pilot must arrive with speed, bend around the well, mine during the pass, and leave before the trajectory collapses.

Deep-field access is not just an upgrade gate. It is a skill gate.

---

## 8. The Base

The base exists in low-gravity open space because nobody sane builds inside the heavy field.

It is a trade depot, repair yard, weighing station, launch point, and social pressure cooker. Everything valuable comes back through it. That makes return trips predictable and dangerous.

A ship returning full of Dead Iron is slow, valuable, and visible. Everyone knows what it means when a scarred miner comes in hot from the deep field with containment pods glowing.

The base should feel practical, exposed, and a little underbuilt for the amount of money passing through it.

---

## 9. Ships

Ships in this world are gravity-field tools.

They are not designed around elegance. They are designed around survival, repairability, and whatever the pilot could afford last week.

Common ship features:

- oversized maneuvering thrusters
- exposed reaction-control nozzles
- reinforced nose cages
- external cargo pods
- modular engine clusters
- patched armor plates
- bolted-on sensor booms
- magnetic collection rings or intake funnels
- heat-scarred braking surfaces
- visible cable runs and field repairs

Upgrades should look like physical additions. A better ship is not just numerically stronger. It visibly carries its history.

A new ship looks stripped and desperate. A veteran ship looks busy, scarred, and specific.

---

## 10. Other Pilots and Enemies

The field is occupied.

Other ships are there for the same reason the player is: Dead Iron.

Some mine honestly. Some patrol corporate claims. Some wait near dangerous wells for better pilots to make mistakes. Some chase distress pings. Some collect spilled cargo from wrecks. Some are just desperate.

Enemy behavior does not need a complicated moral framework. The world is enough:

- Dead Iron is valuable.
- Full ships are vulnerable.
- Wrecked ships spill cargo.
- The base is predictable.
- The deep field rewards greed.

This makes conflict feel natural without turning the game into a pure dogfighter.

Combat is still a gravity problem. Projectiles curve. Ships overshoot. A bad shot can become a good shot if the field bends it. A chase near a heavy asteroid is chaotic because nobody is flying through neutral space.

---

## 11. Death and Salvage

When a ship dies, its cargo does not simply disappear.

Containment fails. Pods rupture. Dead Iron fragments scatter into the local field, becoming floating pickups, dangerous debris, or valuable salvage.

This supports the recovery-loop fiction:

A death creates a temporary salvage site.

The player can return to reclaim their cargo. Enemies can get there first. The field itself may pull scattered ore into new patterns. A failed run leaves a mark, at least for a while.

Dead Iron wants to collect. Everyone else wants to collect it first.

---

## 12. Sound and Feedback

Dead Iron should be felt before it is explained.

The closer the ship gets to a strong deposit, the more the world should communicate strain:

- low hull groan
- subsonic rumble
- cockpit vibration
- controller rumble
- loose cockpit objects rattling
- containment pod hum changing pitch as cargo increases
- warning tones that feel mechanical, not sterile
- silence snapping back in open space

The sound of the field is part of the HUD.

A skilled player should eventually know when they are too close because the ship sounds wrong.

---

## 13. Names and Language

The world should use plain, workmanlike names more than polished sci-fi terms.

Good naming direction:

- Dead Iron
- Anchor Metal
- wellstone
- black nickel
- dead core
- heavy rock
- well field
- bad orbit
- hot pass
- soft capture
- cargo bloom
- containment flicker
- deep run
- claim rock
- wreck drift

Avoid overly clean technobabble unless a specific character or corporation would use it.

A miner should not say, "The exotic gravimetric substrate is destabilizing."

A miner should say, "Pods are singing. We are too heavy."

---

## 14. Design Principles from the Story

This story exists to support the game, not to decorate it.

The fiction should reinforce these rules:

1. **Gravity is the main terrain.**  
   The player is not flying through empty space with rocks in it. They are flying through a field of forces.

2. **Mining requires closeness.**  
   The resource is collected by skilled navigation, not by standing still and holding a button.

3. **The best resources are the most dangerous.**  
   Richer asteroids have stronger gravity because both come from Dead Iron concentration.

4. **Cargo changes the return trip.**  
   A successful mining run creates new vulnerability. The trip home matters.

5. **Ships should show their function.**  
   Every upgrade should feel like a physical answer to a problem the field creates.

6. **The world should feel used.**  
   This is not pristine science fiction. It is a working, worn, risky economy.

7. **Mystery is allowed, but the rules should be clear.**  
   Nobody has to fully understand Dead Iron. The player only needs to understand how it behaves.

---

## 15. Short Version

The asteroid field contains Dead Iron, a rare gravitational ore that becomes dangerous when concentrated. Some asteroids contain buried Dead Iron cores, creating gravity wells strong enough to bend ship trajectories. Mining requires close-pass extraction: pilots skim exposed deposits, collect ore fragments, and store them in powered containment pods that keep the material dispersed and stable. The richest asteroids are the most dangerous because resource value and gravitational pull come from the same source.

The player is an independent pilot working this field from a rough base in open space. Runs are about going deeper, riding gravity wells, collecting Dead Iron, surviving the return, upgrading the ship, and learning how close is too close.

The game is about momentum, greed, control, and the strange pleasure of using danger as a tool.
